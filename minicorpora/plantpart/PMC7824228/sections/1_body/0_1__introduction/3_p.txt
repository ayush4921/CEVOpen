0: Curcuma amada Roxb.
1: is a perennial, rhizomatous herb growing up to about 80 cm in height and is morphologically similar to  Curcuma longa species, known as turmeric.
2: C. amada is distributed in the wild and cultivated in different parts of India, Myanmar, and Thailand [ 11].
3: In Myanmar, it is planted in the backyard and small-scale farming for domestic use and home consumption in the local markets.
4: C. amada rhizome is the most valued part of the plant which contains a variety of terpenoids, flavonoids, and phenylpropanoids [ 6].
5: It is used not only as a food ingredient but also as a source of alternative medicine for treating digestive disorders such as indigestion, stomach pain, loss of appetite, constipation, colic, and hiccups, and to cure internally bronchitis, asthma, cough, and skin disorders, including itching and inflammation [ 8, 11, 12].
6: It has been used in the Ayurveda and Unani medicinal systems as a diuretic, laxative, appetizer, antipyretic, aphrodisiac, emollient, and expectorant [ 9].
