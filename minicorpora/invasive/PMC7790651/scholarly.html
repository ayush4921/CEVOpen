Abstract
Abstract

Plantâ€"fungal associations are frequently key drivers of plant invasion success. Foliar fungi can benefit their invasive hosts by enhancing growth promotion, disease resistance and environmental stress tolerance. However, the roles of foliar fungi may vary when a given invasive plant faces different stresses. In this study, we designed three independent experiments to evaluate the effects of a foliar fungus, Colletotrichum sp., on the growth performance of the invasive plant Ageratina adenophora under different soil conditions, as well as the responses of A.Â adenophora to the foliar fungal pathogen Diaporthe helianthi and to herbivory. We found that the soil type was the most influential factor for the growth of A.Â adenophora. The role of the foliar fungus Colletotrichum sp. varied in the different soil types but generally adversely affected leaf development in A.Â adenophora. Colletotrichum sp. may be a weak latent foliar pathogen that can enhance the pathogenicity of D.Â helianthi on leaves of A.Â adenophora and marginally reduce signs of herbivory by natural insects in the wild on A.Â adenophora seedlings. In general, the benefits of the foliar fungus Colletotrichum to the fitness of A.Â adenophora are not significant in the context of this experimental design. However, our data highlight the need to consider both aboveground and belowground biota in different soil habitats when evaluating the effects of foliar fungi.

Soil type played the most important role in the growth of A.Â adenophora, and the role of the foliar fungus Colletotrichum sp. varied according to different habitats but in general adversely affected the growth of A.Â adenophora. The Colletotrichum sp. might be a weak latent pathogen and enhanced the pathogenicity of Diaporthe helianthi on leaves of A.Â adenophora. Colletotrichum sp. marginally reduced the feeding frequency of seedlings by natural enemies in the wild.

INTRODUCTION

The rapid expansion of exotic plants has caused serious damage to the structure and function of invaded ecosystems and has resulted in enormous social and economic losses (Richardson &amp; Ricciardi,Â 2013; VilÃ etÂ al.,Â 2010). Determining why invasive plants succeed in their introduced range has been a major goal of invasion ecology. Plantâ€"fungal associations are frequently key drivers of plant invasion success; a fungal species can act as a mutualist by enhancing plant defense, growth and stress tolerance or as a pathogen to cause establishment failure (Dickie etÂ al.,Â 2017; Flory &amp; Clay,Â 2013). Foliar fungi are diverse in terrestrial ecosystems, including endophytes and epiphytes, and are often assumed to be mutualists, latent saprotrophs or pathogens of plants (Arnold,Â 2007; Osono etÂ al.,Â 2004; Schulz etÂ al.,Â 1999). Foliar fungi have also been observed to be capable of altering plant disease severity (Arnold etÂ al.,Â 2003; Busby etÂ al.,Â 2016). For exotic plants, foliar fungi can assist the host to grow, resist disease, and change herbivory. For example, Aschehoug etÂ al.Â (2012) indicated that the inoculation of endophytic Alternaria (CID120) on the invasive forb Centaurea stoebe could directly increase its growth. Currie etÂ al.Â (2019) demonstrated that foliar fungi (Colletotrichum acutatum, Alternaria alternata and Cladosporium oxysporum) appeared to mitigate the effect of the pathogen Puccinia komarovii on the invasive weed Impatiens glandulifera. Rudgers and Clay (2008) showed that the foliar fungus Neotyphodium coenophialum could significantly reduce the abundance and diversity of herbivorous arthropods on the invasive plant Lolium arundinaceum.

In addition to aboveground biota, invasive plants also interact with belowground biota. Escape from soilâ€�specific host pathogens has been suggested to play a key role in determining the success of exotic plant invasion into local ecosystems, and this phenomenon is referred to as positive plantâ€"soil feedback (PSF) (Callaway etÂ al.,Â 2004; Hawkes etÂ al.,Â 2005; Klironomos,Â 2002; Kulmatiski etÂ al.,Â 2008). Nonetheless, exotic plants have not experienced uniformly positive PSF, for example, Andonian etÂ al.Â (2011) indicated that an invasive weed experienced negative PSF; Crawford and Knight (2017) found that Lespedeza cuneata experienced positive PSF in invaded soils only in the absence of competition; and for some plant species, a positive PSF was observed in a glasshouse but not in the field ((Schittko etÂ al.,Â 2016). These discrepancies suggest that the PSF experienced by the exotic invaders is context dependent and may involve the soil habitat type in which the plants grow (Pizano etÂ al.,Â 2019).

More interestingly, interactions of belowground and aboveground biota may exist when performing PSF studies. For example, the invasive plant I.Â glandulifera experienced a positive PSF, and the effect of the PSF even extended beyond the soil microbial community to affect foliar fungi, which in turn enhanced resistance to herbivory in I.Â glandulifera and thus accentuated the invasive properties of this species (Pattison etÂ al.,Â 2016). In reality, invasive hosts expand into different habitats across a large geographical scale (VilÃ etÂ al.,Â 2010) and potentially interact with diverse belowground biota (e.g., soilborne pathogens) and aboveground biota (e.g., foliar fungi). However, it is unclear whether the invasive host experiences distinct soil biota effects in different habitats and how the roles of the soil biota change after a foliar fungus is introduced; in addition, it is also unknown whether a foliar fungus can modify both aboveground diseases and herbivory in the invasive host.

The invasive plant Ageratina adenophora (Sprengel) R. M. King and H. Robinson is a perennial herb of the Compositae family that is native to Central America. The spread of A.Â adenophora is considered to be a severe problem in more than 30 countries in Asia, Africa, Oceania, Europe and North America (Datta etÂ al.,Â 2019). In China, this weed has been reported to reduce the diversity of native plant species, crop production in agricultural land and forage production in pastures; moreover, this weed is poisonous to domestic animals (Poudel etÂ al.,Â 2019). Therefore, understanding the mechanisms of successful weed invasion is ecologically and economically important. Previous results have indicated that the roles of soil biota in the establishment of A.Â adenophora are habitat dependent, for example, Niu etÂ al.Â (2007) indicated that soil biota collected from mixed evergreenâ€�broadleafâ€�deciduous forests had strong inhibitory effects on A.Â adenophora growth; however, Xiao etÂ al.Â (2014) indicated that sterilization had no significant effect on A.Â adenophora biomass growing in soils collected from a tropical botanical garden. Nevertheless, once established, A.Â adenophora can change the soil biota to promote its own growth (Niu etÂ al.,Â 2007), for example, by increasing the abundance of nitrogenâ€�fixing bacteria (Xu etÂ al.,Â 2012) and root endophytic Enterobacter bacteria (Chen etÂ al.,Â 2019). Moreover, A.Â adenophora has also been reported to host various foliar fungi, including pathogens (Poudel etÂ al.,Â 2019; Zhou etÂ al.,Â 2010) and endophytes (Mei etÂ al.,Â 2014, Fang et al., 2019). In addition, foliar herbivory was frequently observed in our routine investigations. These data suggest the occurrence of potential aboveground and belowground biotic interactions with A.Â adenophora, which remain to be characterized. In this study, we designed three independent experiments to determine the effect of a foliar fungus, Colletotrichum, on the growth performance of A.Â adenophora in different soil conditions, as well as the functional responses of A.Â adenophora to aboveground pathogenic fungi and herbivores.

2.2
Inoculation and detection of foliar fungi

The fungus JK99 was previously isolated from healthy leaves of A.Â adenophora, making up nearly 30% of the total isolated fungi (Mei etÂ al.,Â 2014). It has been identified as Colletotrichum sp. (see colony morphology in FigureÂ S1. Its ITS gene was sequenced and was completely identical to the sequence of a strain of Colletotrichum sp. A285 that we previously submitted to GenBank with an accession number of MK247540). Calcium carbonate medium (1,000Â ml of distilled water containing 30Â g of CaCO3, 20Â g of glucose and 18Â g of agar) was used for sporulation at 28Â°C. The preparation of the spore suspension and inoculation were performed as previously reported (Arnold etÂ al.,Â 2003). Briefly, the spores produced on the plate were washed with 0.5% sterilized gel solution (100Â ml of distilled water containing 0.5Â g of gel). The mycelium was removed with sterile filter paper, and the spore suspension was diluted to 106Â CFU/ml using a haemocytometer. Then, the A.Â adenophora leaves were misted to saturation on both the upper and lower surfaces using this spore suspension with a sterile sprayer, and the control group was sprayed with sterile 0.5% gel solution. The whole plant was wrapped in a sterile black plastic bag for 48Â hr to promote fungal infection, and the fungal infection was detected after 15Â days, according to Arnold etÂ al.Â (2003). The leaves inoculated and not inoculated (control plants) with Colletotrichum sp. (named Col+ and Colâˆ’, respectively) were surface sterilized by submerging in 0.5% sodium hypochlorite for 2Â min and 70% ethanol for 2Â min and rinsing with sterile water 3 times, and then the surfaceâ€�sterilized leaves were cut into fragments of approximately 6 mm2. The number of leaf fragments with fungi and the colony morphology were observed and recorded. The fungal infection rate of the leaves (Col+ and Colâˆ’) was calculated by dividing the number of fragments with fungi by the total number of fragments.

2.5
Herbivory experiment

DISCUSSION

Local soil biotic and abiotic factors ultimately determine the positive or negative effects of PSF on plants (Bennett &amp; Klironomos,Â 2019). Our report characterized the growth response of the invasive plant A.Â adenophora when grown in agricultural and forest soils upon interaction with a foliar fungus. We found that the soil source played the most important role in the growth of A.Â adenophora. Although the forest soil showed significantly higher levels of macronutrients, such as N, P, and K, than the agricultural soil (TableÂ 2), on average, all measurements for A.Â adenophora growth were lower in forest than in agricultural soil (FigureÂ 4). Because the soil, as an inoculation source, was mixed with a sterilized matrix at a volume ratio of 1:9 and watered with Hoagland nutrient solution during the growth of A.Â adenophora (see Methods), the effects of the macronutrients in the soils on A.Â adenophora were likely lessened. These data thus reflected that the unmeasured micronutrients in the agricultural soil still contributed to the growth of A.Â adenophora. For example, Zhang etÂ al.Â (2004) found that A.Â adenophora tends to enrich soil selenium.

Regarding the soil biota effects, we found that sterilization caused declines in most measures of A.Â adenophora growth in forest soil, including LDB per unit area, ADB and SL, and conversely, improved the LDB per unit area in agricultural soil (FigureÂ 4). These findings suggested that A.Â adenophora experienced positive soil biota effects in forest soil but negative or neutral effects in agricultural soil, supporting previous results that soil biota from different habitats have distinct inhibitory effects on A.Â adenophora growth (Niu etÂ al.,Â 2007; Xiao etÂ al.,Â 2014). The negative feedback in agricultural soil indicates that heavy loads of pathogens exist in these soils (Ashizawa etÂ al.,Â 2010; Etebu &amp; Osborn,Â 2010; Kohn,Â 1995). In contrast, the positive effect of forest soils on A.Â adenophora growth may be related to the high abundance and/or diversity of beneficial microbes, for example, AM fungi, in intact forest soils (Bordoloi etÂ al.,Â 2015). The invasiveness of a given plant and the invasibility of the ecosystem together determine the successful colonization of exotic plants in nonâ€�native habitats (Milbau &amp; Nijs,Â 2004). Therefore, the positive feedback in forest soil does not indicate that A.Â adenophora invades this habitat more easily than agricultural soils, which may contain unknown nutrients that support A.Â adenophora growth better than forest soils. Great variation can be observed even among agricultural soils (Franklin &amp; Mills,Â 2003) based on many factors, and the same is true for forest soils (Yang etÂ al.,Â 2018). Therefore, a variety of factors need be ruled out before multiple soil types in both groups can be used in experiments. Nonetheless, the combined effects of both soil biota and nutrient limitation must be considered in evaluations of the invasiveness of species and the invasibility of ecosystems.

Interestingly, inoculation with the foliar fungus Colletotrichum sp. changed the growth response of A.Â adenophora in different soil types, for example, it decreased the aboveground biomass of A.Â adenophora in nonsterilized forest soils but had no effect on A.Â adenophora growth in agricultural soils, whether sterilized or nonsterilized (FigureÂ 4b). Moreover, this foliar fungus mainly affected aboveground rather than belowground growth, in particular by decreasing the LDB per unit area of A.Â adenophora in both sterilized and nonsterilized forest and agricultural soils (FigureÂ 4a). Similarly, Newcombe etÂ al.Â (2009) demonstrated that the foliar fungus Fusarium (CID107) inhibited the development of C.Â stoebe leaves and led to a decrease in aboveground biomass. Our data indicate that a given foliar fungus can have a complex interaction with hosts and impact different biological traits of hosts depending on the soils of the habitat and the presence of soil biota.

Many studies have verified that foliar fungi can help invasive host plants resist pathogens; for example, Currie etÂ al.Â (2019) demonstrated that fungal endophytes appeared to be antagonistic to rust fungus (Puccinia komarovii). In this study, we found that the foliar fungus Colletotrichum sp. promoted the pathogenicity of D.Â helianthi on A.Â adenophora leaves (FigureÂ 5a). Moreover, when compared with leaves without Colletotrichum sp. inoculation, the spot area formed by D.Â helianthi on leaves inoculated with Colletotrichum sp. developed faster with increasing leaf age (FigureÂ 5b). These results suggest the existence of a synergism between these two fungi that causes them to be more virulent to A.Â adenophora. Coinfection is common in nature and usually increases the pathogenicity of pathogens to the host (Laine,Â 2011). For example, coinfection with Verticillium dahliae and Colletotrichum coccodes causes more severe foliar disease symptoms and crown rot in potato (Nicola) than inoculation with either of the two pathogens separately (Tsror &amp; Hazanovsky,Â 2001).

In addition, although Colletotrichum sp. JK99 has been isolated from healthy leaves and reported as an endophyte of A.Â adenophora (Mei etÂ al.,Â 2014), we found that healthy Col+ leaves developed a larger leaf spot area than healthy Colâˆ’ leaves only through puncturing (FigureÂ 5d), and symptoms worsened with leaf age (FigureÂ 5e). This finding confirms that the foliar fungus Colletotrichum sp. is in fact a latent pathogen of A.Â adenophora, as previously reported for other invasive plant species (de Macedo etÂ al.,Â 2013; Newcombe etÂ al.,Â 2009) and supports the viewpoint that latent pathogens induce disease symptoms when plant tissues are physically damaged (Viret &amp; Petrini,Â 1994). Colletotrichum has various life habits and can be broadly categorized as necrotrophic, hemibiotrophic, latent or quiescent and endophytic (De Silva etÂ al.,Â 2017). Some endophytic fungi, such as latent pathogens and quiescent pathogens, may induce disease symptoms later when the plant tissues age or become weakened (Kogel etÂ al.,Â 2006; Viret &amp; Petrini,Â 1994). Indeed, very recently, our group indicated that many members of Colletotrichum sp. were asymptomatic latent pathogens of both A.Â adenophora and coâ€�occurring native plants (Chen etÂ al.,Â 2020). Nonetheless, the disease symptoms elicited by Colletotrichum sp. were very weak, and disease development was halted within two weeks after wounding (FigureÂ 5f).

Foliar fungi, as a secondary metabolite source for plants, can provide their hosts with antiherbivore defenses (Nisa etÂ al.,Â 2015). A recent review indicates that fungal endophytes commonly have a negative effect on insect herbivores (Gange etÂ al.,Â 2019). Our data also showed that inoculation with Colletotrichum sp. marginally reduced the signs of herbivory on intact A.Â adenophora seedlings by natural insect enemies in the wild (FigureÂ 6). The plant pathogen Colletotrichum sp. has previously been reported to cause extensive mortality in an insect herbivore by directly colonizing insect tissue (Graziosi &amp; Rieske,Â 2015). Similarly, the members of Colletotrichum have been observed to infect scale insects (Marcelino etÂ al.,Â 2008). However, it is not known whether Colletotrichum species produce defense compounds to directly kill insects. Here, it was also unclear how Colletotrichum sp. JK99 modifies the herbivory of A.Â adenophora.

In conclusion, our preliminary study characterized the interactions among an invasive plant, a foliar fungus and natural enemies aboveground and belowground. We found that the soil type played the most important role in the growth of A.Â adenophora, and the role of the foliar fungus, Colletotrichum sp., varied according to the habitat but primarily adversely impacted leaf development in A.Â adenophora. The foliar fungus Colletotrichum sp. might be a latent pathogen, and it enhanced the pathogenicity of D.Â helianthi on A.Â adenophora leaves; this fungus also marginally reduced the signs of herbivory on seedlings from natural insect enemies in the wild. Previously, invasive hosts have been shown to benefit from the presence of foliar fungi (Aschehoug etÂ al.,Â 2012; Currie etÂ al.,Â 2019; Evans,Â 2008; Xiao etÂ al.,Â 2012). The adverse impacts in this study may be related to the latent pathogenic effects of this foliar fungus but also reflect the fact that the ecological functions of foliar fungi are usually context dependent (Hawkes &amp; Connor,Â 2017). In particular, our study highlights the need to consider both aboveground and belowground biota in different habitats when evaluating the effects of foliar fungi.
