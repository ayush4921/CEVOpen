Abstract
Abstract

The ovariectomized rat is a widely used preclinical model for studying postmenopausal and its complications. In this study, the therapeutic effect of flaxseed oil on the ovariectomized adult rats was investigated. Our results showed that biochemical parameters including calcium, oestrogen and progesterone levels increase 8Â weeks after ovariectomy in rats. Also, the amount of alkaline phosphatase decreased significantly after 8Â weeks compared with the OVX rat. The healing potential of flaxseed oil was proven by successfully recovering the affected tissue and preventing the unpleasant symptoms of ovariectomized rats. The biological effects of flaxseed oil may be due to high amounts of fatty acids, phytoestrogens and an array of antioxidants. The results suggest that flaxseed oil can mimic the action of oestrogen and can be a potential treatment for hormone replacement therapy (HRT).

  1. The potential use of flaxseed oil as the natural source for hormone replacement therapy on the ovariectomized rat model was investigated.

  2. The antioxidant properties and biochemical parameters including alkaline phosphatase, calcium, estrogen, and progesterone levels were determined in ovariectomy rats.

INTRODUCTION

Ovariectomy (OVX) regarded as one of the most common surgical procedures in the world with approximately one third of women all around the world (Laughlinâ€�Tommaso etÂ al.,Â 2014). These signs and symptoms of ovariectomy include fever, redness, swelling, vomiting, urinating difficulty, chronic abdominal pain, shortness of breath or chest pain, mood swings as well as deficiency of ovarian hormones (Coleman etÂ al.,Â 2011; Kurita etÂ al.,Â 2016). Ovarian hormones play a pivotal role in maintaining the integrity and function of female genital tissue structure, and shown to enhance genital sensation, maintain blood flow, rugae of the vaginal wall as well as vaginal lubrication (Traish etÂ al.,Â 2018; Tzur etÂ al.,Â 2016). OVX alongside its diverse effects on alterations of lipid metabolism and loss of oestrogen signalling also results in an increased risk of heart disease, atherosclerosis, obesity, inflammation and osteoporosis (Leung, 2012). As a result, developing new candidate or improving diet by modifying the amount and type of fat ingested, which in turn improves lipid profile without side effects, have raised the prospect of new intervention with a much lower cost than drugs.

Substantial, surveys in humans and animals suggested that dietary phytoestrogens have protective effects against menopausal symptoms and a variety of disorders, including cardiovascular disease, cancer, hyperlipidemia, osteoporosis and various forms of chronic renal disease (Bhathena &amp; Velasquez,Â 2002).

Phytoestrogens are found in numerous fruits, seeds and whole grains and are categorized into three classes including the isoflavones, lignans and coumestans. Phytoestrogens with a similar chemical structure to endogenous oestrogen can bind to oestrogen receptors and affect the glucose, lipid metabolism and increase the absorption of calcium (Sirotkin &amp; Harrath,Â 2014) which in return result in improving memory, neuronal survival, reproductive behaviour, emotion and sexual differentiation (Raheja etÂ al.,Â 2018). Some studies also reported the pain modulation role of phytoestrogens (Valsecchi etÂ al.,Â 2008; Wong etÂ al.,Â 2017).

Flaxseed (linseed, Linum usitatissimum, Linaceae) is the seed from the flax plant (Linum usitatissimum L.) and is widely used as a powerful natural product in many parts of the world due to the potential health benefits associated with some of its biologically active components. Flaxseed oil is naturally rich sources of polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA), especially omegaâ€�3 and omegaâ€�6 fatty acids, lignans (secoisolariciresinol diglycosideâ€�SDG), soluble antioxidants and insoluble fibres (Goyal etÂ al.,Â 2014; Ivanov etÂ al.,Â 2011; Singh etÂ al.,Â 2011; Thompson etÂ al., 1996; TourÃ© &amp; Xueming,Â 2010). Flaxseed oil is a safe and healthy supplement that can boost intake of omegaâ€�3 fatty acids from seafood and lower cholesterol as well as chances of developing cardiovascular diseases (Bloedon &amp; Szapary,Â 2004), diabetes, risk of cancer especially the breast and prostate gland (Dabrosin etÂ al.,Â 2002), inflammation (Hallund etÂ al.,Â 2008) and osteoporosis (Elâ€�Saeed etÂ al.,Â 2018). Various studies demonstrated no evidence of acute toxicity of flaxseed oil after 90Â days of oral, dermal and inhalation consumption, and no LD50 was reported (Baker &amp; Grant,Â 2018; Marambe etÂ al.,Â 2017).

The aim of this study was to investigate the efficacy of the flaxseed oil on the lipid profiles, biochemical, hormonal indexes in serum and uterine stereological in ovariectomized (OVX) rats. Flaxseed oil is an exceptionally safe natural product and a rich source of PUFA specifically in Ï‰â€�3 fatty acids, phytoestrogenic lignans and an array of antioxidants. We hypothesized that flaxseed oil administration as a nutritious meal would regulate the biochemical and hormonal pathway and have a positive impact on the ovariectomized rats as a model of human menopause.

DISCUSSION

The purpose of this study was to reduce the complications of menopause and postâ€�ovarian problems in women. Ovariectomy is well known to decreased oestrogen hormones, calcium metabolism, bone mineral content, endometrial and lumen weight and volume. These diverse effects could also result in bone fracture tissue called osteoporosis (Lello etÂ al.,Â 2018; Sattar, 2016; Taylor etÂ al.,Â 2019). The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved OVX rat model to study menopause, postmenopausal osteoporosis and the possible natural and synthetic therapeutic agents (Johnston &amp; Ward,Â 2015).

The main issue is that oestrogen and progesterone changes due to ovariectomy and alter biochemical factors including alkaline phosphate and calcium level (Ghazvini etÂ al.,Â 2016; Shen etÂ al.,Â 2017). Oestrogen is lipophilic steroid hormone that binds to nuclear oestrogen receptors (ERÎ± and ERÎ²), regulates gene expression within DNA, activates gene transcription and stimulates steroidal hormone (GonzÃ¡lezâ€�Bengtsson etÂ al.,Â 2016). Additionally, oestrogens can activate the cell surface Gâ€�protein coupled Oestrogen Receptor (GPER) which promotes cyclic AMP (cAMP) and lesser intracellular Ca2+ (Nilsson etÂ al.,Â 2011; Sharma etÂ al.,Â 2018). However, the exact contributions of oestrogen signalling through ERÎ±, ERÎ² or GPER related to lipid and lipoprotein metabolism are not well defined (Palmisano etÂ al.,Â 2018). In return, the loss of oestrogens in adult animals is associated with body weight gain and the development of obesity (Chen etÂ al.,Â 2009). Moreover, deficiency of oestrogen reduces thiol antioxidant defences in bone cells and the expression of TNFÎ±, which prompt apoptosis and causes loss of bone (Jagger etÂ al.,Â 2005; Lean etÂ al.,Â 2003). Also due to the reduction in ovarian hormones, uterine structure and ovarian thickness changes and as a result endometrial atrophy happened. Another problem in ovariectomized women is oxidant/antioxidant imbalance that can exacerbate their wellâ€�being. There is increasing evidence suggesting the destructive role of free radicals oxidative damage on cells and bone (Muthusami etÂ al.,Â 2005). Menopause happens due to the loss of ovarian follicular function, decreases follicleâ€�stimulating hormone and oestrogen levels. These may result in bone turnover, urogenital and menstrual cycle disorders, vasomotor changes and unpleasant symptoms such as irritability, arthralgias, sleep disturbances and mood swings (Das,Â 2002). However, the exact mechanism of bone metabolism by oestrogens is not clear. The protective effect of oestrogens on bone mass maintenance may be the result of nonnuclear initiated signalling of the ERÎ± (Almeida etÂ al.,Â 2017). H2O2 generated in the mitochondria of osteoclasts is required for the loss of cortical bone mass caused by oestrogen or androgen deficiency (Ucer etÂ al.,Â 2015, 2017). As a result consumption of high amounts of antioxidants can markedly improve functions in ovariectomized cases.

For centuries flaxseed, has been cultivated considering its potential health benefits and medicinal purposes. In recent decades, flaxseed oil has been studied for many potential uses including kidney disorders, antiâ€�inflammation, bacterial and fungal infection, diabetes, cardiovascular diseases and cerebrovascular stroke (Goyal etÂ al.,Â 2014; Madhusudhan,Â 2009).

Moneim et al showed that consumption of 1,000Â mg/kg flaxseed oil in rats not only depicted no toxicity but also limit the renal cytotoxicity (Abdel Moneim etÂ al.,Â 2011). The other studies also confirm the potential protective influence of flaxseed oil against renal toxicity through alteration biochemical and histopathological factors (Shaikh Omar,Â 2018). These data confirmed the low toxicity and therapeutic potency of the mentioned oil.

The high interest in consumption of flaxseed oil as a medicinal and nutritional product is mainly due to its high PUFA content (especially omegaâ€�3 and omegaâ€�6 groups), lignans, highâ€�quality proteins, fibres and carbohydrates. Although the composition of flaxseed can vary with genetics, growing environment, seed processing and method of analysis (Bernacchia etÂ al.,Â 2014; Rubilar etÂ al.,Â 2010), PUFAs are a family of lipids identified by the position of the last double bond in their structure. Several studies confirmed that flaxseed oil is a rich source of essential PUFA such as linoleic acid and Î±â€�linolenic acid which regulate prostaglandin synthesis and, hence, enhance the woundâ€�healing process. Besides omegaâ€�3, characterized as the most known PUFA, improve the development of the nervous system, reducing cardiovascular disease and cancer. Omegaâ€�3 is also known to lessen the severity and minimize symptoms of chronic inflammatory and inflammatory bowel disease. However, direct evidence of potentially beneficial effects of dietary PUFA on human osteoporosis is still lacking. Omegaâ€�3 fatty acids consumption reduces plasma triglycerides (TG) levels, and small dense LP while increasing large buoyant LDL and HDLâ€�C, may result in an improved lipid profile (Pirillo &amp; Catapano,Â 2013). The role of omegaâ€�3 fatty acid supplementation in dyslipidemia treatment is also shown in other studies (Maki etÂ al.,Â 2009; Saito etÂ al.,Â 2008; Tanaka etÂ al.,Â 2008).

The primary investigation depicted that in ovariectomized female rats, the bone weight of femur and tibia decreased significantly. Interestingly, the PUFAâ€�enriched diet prevented the loss of bone weight and strength caused by oestrogen deficiency, as would occur in women during postâ€�ovary problems (Sakaguchi etÂ al.,Â 1994). In another research project, oil containing a high amount of PUFA acts as antagonistic to arachidonic acid with prostanoid action, which conserves and also stimulate bone mineral content in adult especially during oestrogen deficiency (Watkins etÂ al.,Â 2003).

Different mechanism explains the synergic effect of oestrogen and PUFAs. First, the beneficial actions of oestrogen depend on PUFA, specifically, PUFA increases the sensitivity of receptors towards oestrogen and can modulate the binding of hormones (even at very low concentration) to their nuclear receptors and produced appropriate and enough response. As a result, when the levels of PUFAs decrease, the binding of oestrogen to its receptors will not be optimum, leading to suboptimal responses (Das,Â 2002). Second, oestrogen and PUFAs mostly interact with each other in such a way that the action and regulation of one depend on other molecules. The correlation between them could be enhanced during the overproduction of NO which plays an important role in atherosclerosis, osteoporosis, neurodegenerative conditions and memory (Das,Â 2002; Van der Kraan etÂ al.,Â 2000). PUFAs may be key to the actions of oestrogen due to altering the expression of receptors on the cells. Hence, a combination of PUFAs and oestrogen may be necessary to optimize their benefit and response (Nordoy etÂ al.,Â 1998; NordÃ¸y etÂ al.,Â 2000). Third, PUFAs are involved in the antiâ€�osteoporotic action of oestrogen via mediating some of the actions of TGFâ€�Î² (Newman,Â 1990).

PUFAs also well defined as antiâ€�inflammatory agents through inhibiting the production of proinflammatory cytokines ILâ€�1, ILâ€�2 and TNFâ€�Î± (Das,Â 2000). PUFA modifies the actions of some of the growth factors that influence immune response and decrease free radical generation (Das,Â 2001; Mohan &amp; Das,Â 2000, 2001). Studies have evaluated the biological mechanism of PUFA on bone loss. Recent research highlighted that longâ€�term intake of nâ€�3 PUFA increasing leptin and IGFâ€�1 levels and improved the mechanical properties of cortical bone (Rahman etÂ al.,Â 2009).
