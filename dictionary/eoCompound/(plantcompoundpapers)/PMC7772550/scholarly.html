Abstract

Phytochemical investigations from the flower and leaf extracts of D.Â cochinchinensis resulted in the isolation and structural elucidation of five new polyoxygenated seco-cyclohexene derivatives, desmoscochinchinenes A-E (1â€"5), together with 11 known compounds (6â€"16). The structures on the new compounds were elucidated from their spectroscopic data, including UV, IR, NMR, and HRESITOFMS. Some of the isolated compounds were evaluated for their Î±-glucosidase inhibitory activities. Chrysin (9), pinocembrin 7-O-benzoate (12), and (âˆ’)-(5R)-desmoscochinoxepinone B (16) inhibited Î±-glucosidase better than the standard control (acarbose, IC50 = 83.5 Î¼M) with IC50 values of 5.7, 33.8, 53.3 Î¼M, respectively.

Introduction

Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a chronic metabolic disorder caused by an irregular rise in plasma glucose levels due to the unbalanced development of insulin or unresponsiveness to the influence of this hormone on cellular receptor signal transduction (Stojkovic etÂ al., 2019). There are two underlying causes of diabetes mellitus: type 1 diabetes is a chronic condition that is characterized by the body's inability to produce insulin (Aathira and Jain, 2014), while its incapacity to regulate insulin response is called type 2 diabetes (Skyler etÂ al., 2017). DM has also been reported the cause of many serious health problems, such as eyes (retinopathy), nerves (neuropathy), heart (stroke, coronary heart disease, and peripheral artery disease), teeth and gum problems, cancer, and depression (Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration, 2011). In diabetes treatment, regulation of the absorption rate of blood sugar from the small intestine by inhibiting digestive enzymes, such as Î±-glucosidase, is essential (Kim etÂ al., 2008; Holman etÂ al., 1999). Miglitol, acarbose, and voglibose are well-known commercial drugs used as an inhibitor of Î±-glucosidase in DM control, particularly type 2 diabetes (Saito etÂ al., 1998; Xu etÂ al., 2019). However, long-term use may result in undesirable side effects (Lee etÂ al., 2014). Therefore, it is necessary to find new Î±-glucosidase inhibitors to treat diabetes in the future.

Desmos (Annonaceae) genus, a shrub or climbing plant, is wildly distributed in many Asian countries, including India, Myanmar, Thailand, Philippines, and Malaysia (Kuo etÂ al., 2015) and five species, D.Â chinensis Lour, D.Â cochinchinensis Lour, D.Â crinitus Saff., D.Â dumosus (Roxb.) Saff., and D.Â macrocarpus BÃ¢n have been found in Thailand (Forest Herbarium-BKF, 2012). Desmos cochinchinensis Lour is a medium-sized shrub, which is used as a traditional medicine in South China for the treatment of malaria (Liao etÂ al., 1989; Wu etÂ al., 1994, 1997). There are two different flower forms (large petal and slim petal) of D.Â cochinchinensis (Figure 1) have been grown in Mae Fah University (Latitude: 20Â°04â€²48â€³N; Longitude: 99Â°89â€²43â€³E). Recently, we reported many new flavonoids and oxepinones from the leaf and twig extracts of D.Â cochinchinensis (large petal). Some of them showed interesting Î±-glucosidase inhibitory activity (Meesakul etÂ al., 2019). In continuation of our studies on the different forms of the flower (slim petal) of the same plant, we report herein our studies of the flower and leaf extracts, which resulted in the isolation of 16 compounds (FigureÂ 2), including five previously undescribed compounds. Furthermore, the Î±-glucosidase inhibitory activities of some of the isolated compounds are also reported.

3.1
Structure of the isolated compounds from the flower (slim petal) and leaf extracts of D.Â cochinchinensis

The flower (slim petal) and leaf extracts of D.Â cochinchinensis were individually separated and purified by various chromatography techniques, which led to the isolation and identification of five new polyoxygenated seco-cyclohexenes (1â€"5), together with 11 known compounds (6â€"16). Two new compounds 1 and 2 were obtained from the leaf extract, while three new compounds 3â€"5 were obtained from the flower extract. The known compounds were characterized by the comparison of their physical properties and spectroscopic data with the previous reports and identified as flexuvarin B (6) (Hsu etÂ al., 2016), flexuvarin C (7) (Hsu etÂ al., 2016), flexuvarin D (8) (Hsu etÂ al., 2016), chrysin (9) (Meesakul etÂ al., 2017), 5-hydroxy-7-methoxyflavone (tectochrysin) (10) (Righi etÂ al., 2010), pinocembrin (11) (Meesakul etÂ al., 2017), pinocembrin 7-O-benzoate (12) (Hoeneisen etÂ al., 1993), 2â€²-hydroxy-4â€²,6â€²-dibenzyloxychalcone (13) (Drewes and van Vuuren, 2008), zeylenone (14) (Suthiphasilp etÂ al., 2019), 1R,6S-cherrevenol A (15) (Auranwiwat etÂ al., 2019), and (âˆ’)-(5R)-desmoscochinoxepinone B (16) (Meesakul etÂ al., 2019) (FigureÂ 2).

3.2

Only the stable compounds of adequate amounts (3, 6, 7, 9, 11â€"13, and 16) were evaluated for their Î±-glucosidase inhibitory, nitric oxide (NO) production inhibitory, and glucose uptake activities. Discussion

The 1H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectra of the new compounds 1â€"5 displayed the common characteristic resonances of a polyoxygenated seco-cyclohexene skeleton; including those for three olefinic protons at ca. Î´H 6.5â€"7.4 (H-3), Î´H 6.4â€"7.5 (H-4), and Î´H 5.6â€"6.2 (H-5) and one oxymethylene group at ca. Î´H 4.2â€"5.2 (H-1).

Desmoscochinchinene A (1) gave a molecular formula of C16H16O5, as indicated by its NMR and high-resolution electrospray ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry (HRESITOFMS) data ([M + Na]+m/z 311.0901). The 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopic data of 1 (TablesÂ 1 and 2) displayed resonances typical of an of polyoxygenated seco-cyclohexene (one benzoyl unit [(Î´H 8.07 (2H, d, J = 7.4 Hz, H-3â€² and H-7â€²), 7.47 (2H, t, J = 7.4 Hz, H-4â€² and H-6â€²) 7.60, (1H, t, J = 7.4 Hz, H-5â€²)], three olefinic protons [Î´H 6.60 (1H, d, J = 11.5 Hz, H-3), 7.52 (1H, dd, J = 15.2, 11.5 Hz, H-4), 6.26 (1H, dd, J = 15.2, 7.8, H-5], two oxymethylene groups [Î´H 5.00 (2H, s, H-1) and 4.97 (2H, s, H-1)], and one acetoxy group [Î´H 2.08 (3H, s, 7-OCOCH3)]), which were similar to those of flexuvarin B (6) previously isolated from Uvaria flexuosa (Hsu etÂ al., 2016). The major difference was found that a 6-O-acetyl group of flexuvarin B (6) was replaced by an aldehyde group, which displayed the formyl proton resonance at Î´H 9.68, (1H, d, J = 7.8 Hz, H-6). The configuration of the Î"3,4 and Î"4,5 alkene units were assigned as Z (Î"3,4) and E (Î"4,5) based on the magnitude of their coupling constants (3J) of 11.5 and 15.2 Hz, respectively. The heteronuclear multiple bond connectivity (HMBC) correlations of H-6 (formyl proton) to C-5 (Î´C 134.2) and H-4 and H-5 to C-6 (Î´C 193.1) confirmed this assignment. Other HMBC correlations supporting the characterization of compound 1 was shown in FigureÂ 3.

Desmoscochinchinene B (2) gave the same molecular formula, C16H16O5, as desmoscochinchinene A, which showed an ion peak at m/z 311.0895 [M + Na]+ in the HRESITOFMS data. The NMR spectroscopic data (TablesÂ 1 and 2) of compound 2 were almost identical to those of desmoscochinchinene A. The main difference is only the location of the O-acetyl and formyl groups. Compound 2 contained the formyl group at C-7 and O-acetyl group at C-6, whereas in compound 1, these groups were at C-6 and C-7, respectively. These assignments were confirmed by the HMBC correlations between the formyl proton (Î´H 9.68) with C-1 (Î´C 55.1) and H-1 (Î´H 5.21) and H-3 (Î´H 7.48) with C-7 (Î´C 192.5), and H-6 (Î´H 4.97) and the methyl proton of acetoxy group (Î´H 2.16) with carbonyl carbon of acetoxy group (Î´C 170.7). The configuration of the Î"3,4 and Î"4,5 alkene units were assigned as Z based on the magnitude of the coupling constant (3J) of 11.0 Hz. The key HMBC correlations were supported by the structural characterization of compound 2, displayed in FigureÂ 3.

Desmoscochinchinenes C (3), D (4), and E (5) were isolated as colorless viscous oils. Their NMR spectroscopic data (TablesÂ 1 and 2) were similar to those of desmoscochinchinene B (2) except for the lack of resonances for the benzoyl and formyl groups at C-1 and C-7, respectively. The molecular formula, C7H12O3, of compound 3 was deduced from HRESITOFMS, which showed an ion peak at m/z 167.0690 [M + Na]+. The structure of 3 is a simple polyoxygenated seco-cyclohexene containing the same substituent group, hydroxymethyl unit, on C-1 (Î´H 4.28/Î´C 56.3), C-6 (Î´H 4.32/Î´C 56.7), and C-7 (Î´H 4.21/Î´C 63.2). These assignments were supported by the HMBC correlations, as shown in FigureÂ 2. The molecular formula of desmoscochinchinene D (4) (C9H14O4, HRESITOFMS m/z 209.0792 [M + Na]+) indicated compound 4 contained an acetyl unit when compared to that of compound 3. The NMR spectroscopic data of 4 (TablesÂ 1 and 2) displayed an additional acetyl group at Î´H 2.12/Î´C 20.1 and Î´C 170.7. The HMBC correlations between methyl proton of an acetyl group (Î´H 2.12) and H-6 (Î´H 4.82) with the carbonyl carbon of the acetyl unit (Î´C 170.7) confirmed this assignment. The structure of 5, HRESITOFMS m/z 293.0999 [M + Na]+, corresponding to the molecular formula of C13H18O6, was closely related to that of 3. The main differences were found that all hydroxymethyl units in 3 were replaced by acetoxy units [1-OCOCH3 (Î´H 2.09/Î´C 20.4 and Î´C 170.4), 6-OCOCH3 (Î´H 2.07/Î´C 20.5 and Î´C 170.4), and 7-OCOCH3 (Î´H 2.06/Î´C 20.5 and Î´C 170.2)] and H-1 (Î´H 4.76, s), H-6 (Î´H 4.77, d, J = 7.0 Hz), and H-7 (Î´H 4.66, s) shifted to the lower field in 5. The configuration of the Î"3,4 and Î"4,5 alkene units for 3, 4, and 5 were identified according to their 3J coupling constants, which were similar to those of compound 2. All these assignments were supported by HMBC correlations, as shown in FigureÂ 3.

The biosynthetic pathway of polyoxygenated cyclohexene and seco-cyclohexene were proposed from the shikimic acid pathway (Hsu etÂ al., 2016; Macabeo etÂ al., 2017; Suthiphasilp etÂ al., 2019). The primary intermediate A as shown in FigureÂ 4, is proposed as a forerunner based on our results. The enzymatic cleavage at C-6/C-7 would yield compound 3. Selective acetylation at 6-OH of compound 3 or fully acetylation could produce compounds 4 and 5, respectively. Benzoylation of 3 at 1-OH would produce intermediate B, which was further acetylation at 6-OH and followed by oxidation at 7-OH would provide compounds 8 and 2, respectively. On the other hand, acetylation at 7-OH and followed by oxidation at 6-OH would obtain compound 7, and followed by isomerization at Î"4,5 alkene unit could give compound 1, respectively. Multi-steps of oxidation and acylation of intermediate A would reach to compounds 14 and 15, whereas compound 16 would obtain from compound 3 via oxidation, cyclization, and acetylation.
